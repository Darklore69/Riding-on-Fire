Riding on Fire

[ Starfarer’s Tome: Rocket Dynamics ]

Knowledge of the orbits by itself only allows a wanderer of the stars to visit the countless strange places of space in their dreams. To truly reach the celestials and experience the infinite vastness of the void yourself, you must make use of the mighty machines made by mortals to challenge the gods in their lofty abodes - rockets, the wings that power the flight of all starfarers.

Getting anywhere requires a means of movement. Cars and creatures push against the ground to move. Ships push back water. Planes and helicopters push against air to move or gain lift. All forces involve a pair of objects, and a force is necessary for any change in velocity. Wanderers of the void might not be interested in staying stationary or in a single trajectory forever.

You as a starfarer don’t have the luxuries of the spoilt planet-dwellers. You don’t have a wall in space to push against. You must bring this wall - something to push against - along with yourself on your ride. Rockets are vehicles that accelerate by expelling propellant at some velocity.

Saturn V rocket, designed for the Apollo moon missions

Rockets have a long and interesting history. Even before their working principles were laid down formally, rockets came about through technological advancements and engineering which often predate the underlying scientific principles. Initially used in festivals as fireworks, the Chinese later used them in warfare to propel a mass of arrows at great speeds, from the 13 century onwards. These later spread to other places like the Mongol Empire and Europe.

Some of the core principles on which rockets work were formalized by Isaac Newton in 1687 CE, in the Philosophiae Naturalis Principia Mathematica. These are the three famous laws of motion:

  1. First Law: An object retains a constant velocity (which may be zero) as long as there is no net force acting on it.
  2. Second Law: The net force on an object at any instant is the product of its mass with its acceleration at that instant [ F = ma ].
  3. Third Law: Every force occurs in a “pair” - i.e., each force involves two bodies on which the same force acts with equal magnitude but opposite direction.

The first person to suggest the use of rockets in space exploration was Konstantin Tsiolkovsky. The famous Tsiolkovsky rocket equation, published in 1903 in a Russian aviation magazine, is a derivation from the laws of motion, and the laws of conservation of mass-energy and momentum.

dV = Veln(M0/Mf)

  • dV (“delta-V”) - change in mass of a rocket after expending propellant
  • Ve - velocity of the propellant that is expelled from the rocket
  • M0 - initial mass of the rocket
  • Mf - final mass of the rocket
  • MR (M0/Mf) - “mass ratio”, a commonly used metric for amount of propellant

If mathematically inclined, the aspiring spacefarer may derive this equation themselves with simple knowledge of calculus and the laws of motion. The process is simple - conserving the total momentum of the craft and its propellant and then integrating over time. It is nothing the computers of a good spacecraft can’t handle.

Specific impulse is a term that spacefarers will often come across. It is a convenient way to measure the efficiency of a reaction mass engine (i.e. a rocket). This measures how efficiently the engine converts some mass of propellant to some change in velocity. It will later be seen that high propellant efficiency comes at the cost of low energy (and time) efficiency, depending on the mission profile. The formula for specific impulse is as follows:

Isp = Ve / g0

  • Ve - exhaust velocity, as mentioned before
  • g0 - acceleration due to gravity on Earth’s surface; a convenient metric when most spacecraft are launched from Earth

It can be seen that a high exhaust velocity is directly proportional to a higher specific impulse. This means that the greater the velocity you expel your propellant with, the more delta-V your spacecraft gains for the same mass of propellant. This is rather intuitive so far.

The first liquid-fueled rocket was launched in 1926 CE by Robert H. Goddard, who was convinced of the efficiency of the hydrogen+oxygen fuel & oxidizer combination.

Goddard with his 1926 rocket

The distinction between fuel and oxidizer is an important one for chemical rockets - ones based on combustion reactions:

  • Fuel: Substance that combusts to create rapidly expanding gasses
  • Oxidizer: Substance that facilitates the combustion reaction, like oxygen

Various fuels have been used throughout the ages. Solid ones include gunpowder, ZS (zinc-sulfur) propellants, and composite propellants like those based on ammonium nitrate. Liquid ones include hydrogen, methane and hydrazine - liquefied for various reasons like reducing volume. Oxidizers in practice include atmospheric oxygen, LOX (liquid oxygen), nitric acid, hydrogen peroxide, etc.

Both these substances (“fuel” and oxidizer) constitute propellant in chemical-based rockets. However, there is another classification of the components of propulsion that is more fundamental, though not too useful in chemical rockets as will be seen:

  • Fuel: Substance that provides energy for expelling reaction mass (propellant)
  • Propellant: Substance that is expelled to provide a rocket delta-V

Under this classification, the “fuel” in chemical rockets is both fuel and propellant. Similarly, the oxidizer is also both fuel and propellant. This is because the energy is acquired from a reaction between the fuel and oxidizer (thus making them both fuel), while the products released from combustion are expelled as propellant, making them both components of propellant in this classification.

Chemical rockets generally have low specific impulse, and thus your spacecraft can’t get a lot of delta-V even by expending 90% of its mass as propellant.

Endeavour, 2009: one of many examples of gloriously wasteful chemical propulsion

So you think that these chemical rockets commonly used to launch rockets from a planet won’t cut it for your trek through the void? You’ve made the right call. Enter rocket engines with high exhaust velocities.

Contrary to chemical rockets with their measly mileage (“delta-V-age” or “speedage” in our case, if you will), engines with high specific impulse can get a tremendous amount of delta-V from a small amount of propellant. With the incredible distances involved in space travel, these are the engines that make it possible.

An of a high specific impulse drive from the 21st century is the NASA Evolutionary Xenon Thruster (NEXT), a type of ion engine with an exhaust velocity of 40 km/s. It was used in the DART (Double Asteroid Redirection Test) program, which was a means of defense against objects threatening humanity on Earth.

NEXT being tested in a vacuum chamber

Some other examples of these drives are the Variable Specific Impulse Magnetoplasma Rocket (VASIMR) with a predicted exhaust velocity of 50 km/s, and the experimental Dual-Stage 4-Grid (DS4G) electrostatic ion thruster with an exhaust velocity of 210 km/s. And yes, that is kilometers per second. Get used to large numbers or you’ll make a fool of yourself in the spacefaring profession.

Now it would be natural for you to ask why we use chemical rockets at all. But that is where you would differ from a spacefarer who didn’t leave the Tome unfinished.

High Gear and Low Gear - Wait, isn’t this Space?

Most things unfortunately have both advantages and drawbacks. You are probably familiar with the low and high gear of wheeled vehicles like cars and trucks. Though utterly insignificant against the wings of fire that conquer the void, they should serve well enough as an analogy for our current purposes.

Low gear in automobiles gives better acceleration, while high gear maintains better top speed. Simply put, low gear is all about power while high gear is about mileage. “High gear” and “low gear”, while originating from automobiles, are popular in spacecraft circles as informal descriptions of types of drives.

The chemical rockets can be described as low gear, with terrible propellant efficiency but great thrust power that is necessary to lift off from the oppressive gravity of planets like Earth. Meanwhile, ion engines can be called high gear, with great efficiency but requiring lots of energy for a small amount of thrust that wouldn’t be enough for liftoff, but is great for travel in low gravity. In short, if we take two theoretical engines with the same mass ratio and energy sources, we get:

  • Low gear: low specific impulse, high thrust
  • High gear: high specific impulse, low thrust

Some types of spacecraft can “shift gears”, or change their exhaust velocity to suit their current mission. Their power source may or may not remain the same when they do so, but generally the effect of shifting gears is best seen when the energy consumption remains the same. This can be seen by the formula:

T = 2 P / Ve

  • T (thrust) - force that pushes your craft, directly converting to acceleration
  • P (engine power) - energy consumption per unit time of your engine
  • Ve (exhaust velocity) - velocity of your propellant, as seen before

With the concepts shown so far, you have your spacecraft basics covered. But nothing helps cement information better than working examples.

Budget Issues - Energy Version

The type of spacecraft you choose for your space journeys depends on the amount of energy you have available. Trajectories can differ wildly from roundabout transfer orbits that take ages to brachistochrone trajectories that zip through the system caring little for such trivial problems as moving planets and gravity.

For the entirety of the 20th century CE and much of the 21st, space travel was constrained by energy. The order of the day was trying to limit the delta-V of missions as much as possible and conserve power and propellant, with missions having more or less fixed travel times and launch windows. The less delta-V (through burning engines) one took, the better it would be. This was achieved through means such as gravity assists and multiple stages. One big benefit of this type of optimization is being able to send larger payloads.

Trajectories that optimize delta-V have a few characteristics. They wait for favorable launch conditions (like proper planetary positions), and generally only burn their engines for short bursts (compared to the total travel time) when making maneuvers like transfer orbits.

A Hohmann transfer orbit - the beginning of the yellow arc in Earth’s orbit is the starting point, while the end in Mars’s orbit is the destination. Notice how it is necessary to time the mission such that the celestials are at the start and end points at the start and end time of the mission, respectively.

On the other end of the spectrum, like some of the higher-end power-hungry spacecraft used by spacefarers with more money than they can spend, is the type of spacecraft that optimizes for mission time instead of delta-V.

While the differences between delta-V-optimized and time-optimized spacecraft form a spectrum, the mission profiles of spacecraft on the other extreme are radically different from those using transfer orbits to save energy and propellant.

Very high-energy spacecraft use what are popularly called brachistochrone trajectories, coming from the Greek words brakhistos meaning “shortest” and khronos meaning “time”. These spacecraft generally burn their engines for the entire duration (or the majority) of their journey.

These don’t have to care about gravity with their powerful engines, and simply point at where their destination will be when they reach, burn their engines for roughly half the time, and then flip around and burn in the reverse direction to slow down so as to not smack into the destination when they reach. Thus, these are also commonly called “flip & burn” trajectories.

Typical brachistochrone trajectory

There are many cases where the ideal trajectory involves a coasting phase with engines off that might take up as much time as a third of the journey, but the core concept of “flip & burn” holds regardless.
